AI Disarmament

A Policy Framework for Generative AI in Education

Generative AI is reshaping higher education faster than institutions can comfortably respond. Universities need policy that adapts to new tools without losing sight of the primary goal: protecting learning, judgment, and academic development.

Explore the Framework View the Six Pillars

Central Claim

Governance should begin where learning actually happens.

This framework rejects a single blanket rule for every course. Instead, it starts at the course level, allows policy to move upward through departments and faculties, and uses higher-level rules only when more specific guidance is absent.

Why This Matters

Generative AI changes more than efficiency.

Tools such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini can generate text, code, summaries, and analysis in seconds. In education, that creates a serious challenge: prompt-based output can replace the thinking-intensive work that many assignments are meant to develop.

Academic Integrity

Students can submit AI-generated work as if it were their own, making it harder to judge what they actually understand and can independently produce.

Degree Devaluation

If students progress without building foundational skills, a credential may no longer signal the competence that universities are supposed to certify.

Overreliance and Cognitive Debt

Students may generate acceptable output without understanding the concepts, habits, or reasoning processes that the work was meant to cultivate.

The Core Principle

What matters most is whether producing the artifact is itself the objective, or whether the artifact is only a medium for demonstrating higher-order skill.

Appropriate Use

GenAI can be appropriate when the artifact is mainly a medium and the student is being assessed on judgment, synthesis, communication, or professional reasoning.

Learning Undermined

GenAI undermines learning when producing the artifact is itself the objective, especially in prerequisite and foundational courses where practice is what builds competence.

Use With Caution

Some assignments sit between process and output. In those cases, policy should be careful, explicit, and tied closely to the actual learning objective.

Bottom-Up Framework

Policy should rise from course realities, not descend as a uniform rule.

Different disciplines teach different forms of reasoning, and different courses pursue different goals. The professor-student relationship is where the effects of GenAI become visible first, so policy should begin there and move upward only after lower-level experience has shaped it.

Why bottom-up?

Lower-level policies are closer to the learning objective. They are better positioned to distinguish between meaningful assistance, unhelpful substitution, and outright misuse.

Why not top-down?

A one-size-fits-all rule collapses important differences between courses. It can over-restrict valuable uses of AI in one context while under-protecting learning in another.

Tiered Policy Fallback

Specific policy should always beat general policy.

Every level can define a GenAI policy, but lower-level policies take precedence because they are more granular and more closely aligned with the learning objective. Higher-level rules act as fallback guidance, not default replacements for course judgment.

First Check

Course Policy

If the course provides guidance, that is the governing rule for students and instructors.

If Absent

Department Policy

When a course has no policy, users fall back to department-level expectations and standards.

If Still Absent

Faculty Policy

Faculty guidance becomes the reference point when departments have not issued their own rules.

Final Blanket Fallback

Institution-Wide Policy

The university-wide rule should operate only when no more specific policy exists, avoiding a one-size-fits-all approach across all courses.

Why this matters

The fallback model keeps governance broad enough to provide coverage while still preserving the authority of those closest to the learning environment. It protects against policy gaps without forcing every course into the same rule set.

The Six Pillars

The framework treats governance as deliberate educational design.

These six pillars clarify why GenAI policy must be responsive to context, centered on learning objectives, and broader than prohibition alone.

Pillar 1

Not Just Another Calculator

GenAI is not a neutral convenience tool with trivial educational impact. It can reshape how students think, learn, and complete work, so it requires deliberate policy attention.

Pillar 2

Context Matters

AI policy should depend on course structure, discipline, assessment type, and learning objectives. What is appropriate in one course may be harmful in another.

Pillar 3

Policy Must Evolve

Generative AI tools are changing rapidly, and institutional understanding is still developing. Policy should remain adaptable rather than pretending the environment is stable.

Pillar 4

If Use of GenAI Impedes Learning, That Use Is Harmful

The key test is whether AI interferes with the learning process. If it replaces the practice that develops competence, then the use is educationally harmful.

Pillar 5

Governance Is More Than Regulation

Effective governance is not just about banning or permitting AI. It includes policy, incentives, disincentives, education, guidance, and institutional support.

Pillar 6

Professors, Not Cops

Professors are hired to teach, mentor, and assess learning, not to serve as constant AI investigators. Good policy reduces the burden of policing and supports sustainable teaching practice.

Practical Policy Formation

Good governance shapes assignments, incentives, and expectations.

Protecting learning does not require universal prohibition. It requires thoughtful course design that discourages unhelpful AI use while preserving the objectives that matter most.

Translate the pillars into course design

  • Make assignments more personalized so generic AI output is less useful.
  • Use research-oriented tasks that require source judgment and evidence handling.
  • Tie work to lived experience, reflection, or local context when appropriate.
  • Ask students to explain their process, decisions, and revision path.
  • Use real-world and communication-based tasks that foreground audience and judgment.
  • Give students flexibility to pursue topics they care about, increasing authentic engagement.
  • Diversify assessment formats instead of relying on one repeated assignment structure.

Governance is broader than prohibition

Universities should combine policy with guidance, faculty support, transparent expectations, and assignment design choices that reduce misuse without turning every classroom into an enforcement problem.

The aim is not to punish students into compliance. It is to preserve the conditions under which learning, integrity, and meaningful assessment remain possible.

Takeaway

This framework rejects both blind prohibition and blind adoption.

AI Disarmament proposes a practical policy model for universities: protect learning objectives, govern from the course level upward, and use broader institutional policy as fallback when specificity is absent. The goal is not to stop adaptation. It is to make adaptation educationally responsible.
